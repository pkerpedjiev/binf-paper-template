\abstract{\textbf{Motivation:} Atomic force microscopy images of RNA molecules
are necessarily 2D projections of a 3D molecule. They are often used to confirm
the presence of a particular secondary structure in solution by hypothesizing
about its 3D structure and working backwards to show that the AFM imagery could
be consistent with the 3D structure. The motivation for this work is to
automate and quantify this process by matching projections of predicted
tertiary structures to AFM imagery.\\ \textbf{Results:} The ensemble of coarse
grain tertiary structure models we create contain a native-like structure for
all of our test cases. This implies, that for any given tertiary structure,
given enough 2D projections, we should be able to match the correct 2D
projection. We show that this approach yields favorable results given the data
sets present in \ldots \\ \textbf{Availability:} This software is available as part
of the \texttt{ernwin} RNA tertiary structure prediction package \\
\textbf{Contact:} \href{name@bio.com}{name@bio.com}\\ \textbf{Supplementary
information:} Supplementary data are available at \textit{Bioinformatics}
online.}

\section{Introduction}

What conformations does RNA adopt in solution? Does the RNA we designed have
the structure we designed it to have? Does this solution contain RNA folded
into a particular secondary structure? This are some of the questions that
researchers try to answer when using \ac{AFM} to image RNAs in solution. To
answer them, they typically image the RNAs, add a scale bar, and then, by
manual observation, extract inferences about about the splotches of RNA shown
in the developed pictures (CITE MORE PAPERS)~\cite{yu2015novo}. This quality of
the inferences can range from compelling~\cite{yu2015novo} to presentable
(CITE) to wanting~\cite{petkovic2015sequence}. The common thread binding these
different analysis is the lack of a quantitative analysis of the \ac{AFM}
images.

* distortion of the RNA via the cantilever
